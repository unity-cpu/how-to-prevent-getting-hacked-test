Top 5 things that prevent most hacks

  1. Use a password manager and make every password unique.
  2. Turn on MFA/2FA (prefer authenticator app) for email, banking, socials.
  3. Update your OS, browser, and apps automatically.
  4. Think before you click—verify unexpected links/attachments.
  5. Back up important files to the cloud and an external drive.

These five habits block the majority of common attacks like credential stuffing, phishing, and ransomware.

Foundations

How hacks usually happen

Phishing & Social Engineering

Attackers trick you into handing over passwords or installing malware using fake emails, texts, DMs, or sites that look real. Always double‑check the sender and URL, and when in doubt, go directly to the official site.

Weak or Reused Passwords

If one site gets breached, attackers try the leaked password on your other accounts. Unique passwords + multi‑factor authentication block this.

Unpatched Software

Old versions of apps or the OS can have known bugs. Keep automatic updates on so fixes (security patches) install as soon as they’re released.

Malicious Downloads

Cracked software, random “drivers,” and shady browser extensions often carry malware. Only download from trusted sources and official stores.

Insecure Wi‑Fi

Open Wi‑Fi can let others snoop traffic. Use your phone hotspot or a trusted network when logging into important accounts.

Data Leaks & Public Info

Breaches and oversharing help attackers guess answers to security questions. Keep profiles private and avoid using real answers—use passphrases instead.

Threats

How different malware works (plain‑English)

Virus

Attaches to legitimate files and spreads when those files are opened or shared. Good antivirus and regular updates help catch and block it.

Worm

Self‑spreads over networks without user action—often exploiting old vulnerabilities. Keeping systems patched shuts the door.

Trojan

Disguised as something useful (e.g., a “free game” or “driver”) but opens a backdoor for attackers. Only install software from trusted sources.

Ransomware

Encrypts your files and demands payment. Best protection: offline/cloud backups + updates + don’t click suspicious attachments.

Spyware

Secretly monitors your activity, keystrokes, or camera/mic. Watch for unusual pop‑ups, new toolbars, and high CPU usage; run security scans.

Adware

Bombards you with ads or redirects. Remove sketchy extensions and apps; only install from reputable publishers.

Rootkits

Hide deep in the system to avoid detection. Prevention via secure boot, updates, and reputable security software is key.

Botnets

Malware turns devices into “bots” controlled remotely to send spam or attack sites. Keep IoT devices updated and change default passwords.

Scareware

Fake alerts try to panic you into buying junk software. Don’t click; close the tab and run a real security scan instead.

Action

Protection checklist (start here)

  • Turn on automatic updates for your OS, browser, and apps.
  • Use a password manager; change reused passwords to unique ones.
  • Enable MFA/2FA using an authenticator app (avoid SMS when possible).
  • Back up important files to the cloud and an external drive.
  • Lock down your router: change default password; use WPA2/3; update firmware.
  • Remove browser extensions and apps you don’t recognize or use.
  • Set devices to auto‑lock; use biometrics or a strong passcode.
  • Learn to spot phishing (see section below) and verify senders/links.
  • Review privacy/security settings on email and social accounts.
  • For downloads, prefer official stores or vendor sites only.

If you only do a few things today, pick the first five—those provide the biggest risk reduction.

Skills

Spot phishing & fake sites

Check the sender and the URL – small changes (like paypaI.com with a capital I) are common.
Open a new tab and type the site address yourself instead of clicking links. On mobile, long‑press links to preview the URL.
Beware urgent or scary language – “Your account will be deleted in 1 hour!”
Pressure is a red flag. Real companies rarely demand instant action or payment via gift cards/crypto.
Unexpected attachments – even from someone you know
Their account could be compromised. Verify with a separate message or call before opening.
Use official apps and portals – for banks, delivery, school, and work
Sign in through the official app or bookmarked link, not email/text links.
Recovery

Think you’re hacked? Do this now

  1. Disconnect from the internet (Wi‑Fi/ethernet) to stop data exfiltration.
  2. Change passwords for email and key accounts from a clean device. Turn on MFA.
  3. Run a full scan with your security software; remove suspicious apps/extensions.
  4. Restore from clean backups if files are corrupted or encrypted.
  5. Check account activity (logins, forwarding rules, recovery email/phone). Revoke unknown sessions.
  6. Report to your bank, school/work IT, or relevant service. Freeze cards if needed.
  7. Update everything, then reconnect and monitor for unusual behavior.

Avoid paying ransoms—there’s no guarantee. Focus on backups and restoration.
